1

Respublica Littearia

A KNOWLEDGE-BASED CIVIL SOCIETY

Between 1500 and 1800, a revolution in postal communication allowed ordinary men and women to scatter letters across and beyond Europe. This exchange helped knit together what contemporaries called the respublica litteraria, a knowledge-based civil society, crucial to that era’s intellectual breakthroughs, and formative of many modern European values and institutions.

For instance, We can make an attempt of understanding the complexity of this phenomenon taking as example the correspondence of Samuel Hartlib (ca. 1600 – 10 March 1662), German-British polymath and active promoter and expert writer in many fields.

In the following map we tried to visualise Hartlib's correspondence network.

About the map: every path stands for an hypothetic route that a letter went across during its journey from the writer to the recipient (we calculated them using this service). Even if roads have changed in the past centuries, we believe this is a valid alternative to a visualisation that uses only straight lines to make correspondence visible.

Ironically, the exchange of letters which created this community also dispersed the documentation required to study it, posing enormous difficulties for historians of the subject ever since. This is why some items are not visualised on the map.

For each letter we have a set of attached information, called metadata, which comprehend details such as date, sender, recipient, destination, origin, etc.

This data is often very inconsistent.

Sometimes we know about the existance of letters delivered from a particular city of origin, but the destination addresses have been lost and in some other cases it is just the opposite.

Furthermore there are even more complicated examples regarding the existance of a letter without both the origin and destination location, or even without a date.
To reassemble that scattered material and chart the history of that imagined community we need a revolution in digital communications.

This COST Action is dedicated to envisaging the open-access, open-source, transnational digital infrastructure capable of facilitating the radically multilateral collaboration needed to reassemble this scattered documentation and to support a new generation of scholarly methods and research questions.

2

A Research Network

Coordination for better results

COST Action IS1310 Reassembling the Republic of Letters, 1500–1800 is therefore essentially a networking programme. It emerged in response to the expectation that that the ongoing revolution in digital communications technology can solve the scholarly problem created by the evolution of postal communication in the early modern period, thanks to the contribution of various sholars and researchers spread in different research groups.

2.1 Research at National Level

–

Spread all over the coutries involved in the COST Action there are a number of reaserach centers dealing with the digital humanities, and especially with the study of correspondence. Some of those nations have a higher number of research centers then others.

2.2 Coordination

–

Thanks to this COST Action, those research labs are able to connect each others. Doing so they have a chance to bring their finding to an upper level.

2.3 Fostering Collaboration

–

The framework of the COST Action provides the participants with some interesting format of knowledge exchange such as conferences and meetings. Beside those, it is important to highlight that another form of collaboration comes into existence thanks to the subdivision in working groups, where, even if not all the participant countries are involved, the various topics of the Historiographical Agenda are addressed.

In essence, a new scholarly network is needed in order to study the older scholarly network of the early modern period. To sustain this new network, we need a new breed of digital networking tools; and the purpose of this Action is to assemble the network needed to design these tools: a network to devise a networking platform to support a network studying past networks.

COST Action does not found research itself, but provide the environment for adding up to something greater than the sum of individual parts.
