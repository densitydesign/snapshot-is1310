Objectives of the Action

A digital framework for multi-lateral collaboration

Between 1500 and 1800, a revolution in postal communication allowed ordinary men and women to scatter letters across and beyond Europe. This exchange helped knit together what contemporaries called the respublica litteraria, a knowledge-based civil society, crucial to that era’s intellectual breakthroughs, and formative of many modern European values and institutions.

Ironically, the exchange of letters which created this community also dispersed the documentation required to study it, posing enormous difficulties for historians of the subject ever since.

To reassemble that scattered material and chart the history of that imagined community we need a revolution in digital communications.

This COST Action is dedicated to envisaging the open-access, open-source, transnational digital infrastructure capable of facilitating the radically multilateral collaboration needed to reassemble this scattered documentation and to support a new generation of scholarly methods and research questions.

Geographical Extent

A Network of Researchers

COST Action IS1310 Reassembling the Republic of Letters, 1500–1800 is therefore essentially a networking programme. It emerged in response to the expectation that that the ongoing revolution in digital communications technology can solve the scholarly problem created by the evolution of postal communication in the early modern period, thanks to the contribution of various sholars and researchers spread in different research groups.

2.1 Research at National Level

–

Spread all over the coutries involved in the COST Action there are a number of reaserachers dealing with the digital humanities, and especially with the study of correspondence.

In essence, a new scholarly network is needed in order to study the older scholarly network of the early modern period. To sustain this new network, we need a new breed of digital networking tools; and the purpose of this Action is to assemble the network needed to design these tools: a network to devise a networking platform to support a network studying past networks.

COST Action does not found research itself, but provide the environment for adding up to something greater than the sum of individual parts.

Space and Time

–

WG 1 considers how to identify, represent, analyse, and visualize the data on the spatial and chronological dimensions of correspondence networks found in catalogue records, in letter texts, and in prosopographical data on letter writers and recipients.

People and Network

–

WG 2 studies how best to structure and assemble biographical data describing the citizens of the republic of letters, as well as how to structure, analyse, and visualize data on the networks created by epistolary and related forms of learned exchange.

Text and Topics

–

WG 3 focuses on the presentation of letters as images and digital texts, the development of tools for transcribing, annotating, editing, text mining, and topic modelling them, the translation from print to digital form and vice versa, and the use of training schools to help specify a virtual research environment adapted for the study of this material.

Documents and Collections

–

WG 4 will develop means of describing the physical characteristics of letters, of capturing information on their provenance history, and of assembling information on collections of learned correspondence and existing aids for finding them.

Data Exchange and Strategic Planning

–

WG 5 deals with the means of generating standardized digital data of all these kinds in unprecedented quantities, the technical and legal arrangements necessary for exchanging them between participating individuals, projects, and institutions, and the problems of funding and long-term preservation and sustainability.

Visualisation and Communication

–

WG 6 will conceptualize and specify a new generation of visualization tools applicable to all stages in the process of working with epistolary data, and will consider means of communicating both the scholarly and the technical interest of the Action to a variety of different audiences, within and especially beyond the academy.

Worked Example

A KNOWLEDGE-BASED CIVIL SOCIETY

Studying the letters within the archives, a set of information have been carefully collected and some data has been produced, data that comprehend details such as date, sender, recipient, destination, origin, etc.

Those informations are the remarkable result of the meticulous reasearch work done by a number of different scholars, where the close observation of details produces interpretations that are often too accurate and too particular to be succesfully converted into a form that is easy to store and compute - and then visualisated.

This is the reason why those datasets contain variances and inconsistences; they are to be carefully managed and are not (always) to be intended as “errors” since they could give the opportunity to see intriguing new aspect on the matter (of correspondence).

This way of approaching the analysis and visualisation of data is a very uncommon one, especially if we consider the fact that has often to do with things such as standardization, automation and computation.

Dealing correctly with this kind of data-inconsistances is not a precisely straightforward task, just like is not always easy to visualise them with a set of technology that, even if it works just fine when applied to modern data, it can produce misleading pieces of visualisation when historical data is used as input.

A Misleading Visualisation

Map

The map above represents the extent of the Republic of Letters as if the letters would have travelled nowadays.

Of course, the original routes of the letters were totally different, but we do not have any way of visualising them with today’s resources and technologies.

Moreover, this example of visualisation could be seen as a very misleading one. The reason it that it assumes that the standard way of linking these places involved is overland transportation; but this is not the case. The standard route for a letter from London to Stockholm, for instance, would not be to take the channel tunnel from Dover to Calais and then the road bridges from Germany via Denmark to Sweden: it would travel by down the Thames, up the North Sea, through the Danish Sound, and up the Baltic to Stockholm. In fact, a letter from Transylvania would typically come via Upper Hungary (Slovakia), down the Vistula to Danzig / Gdansk, and likewise via the Baltic and North Sea to London.

So mapping transportation via modern road networks is not sufficient. Compared with another kind of transportation, like the modern air routes, it could be seen, in some ways, even more misleading, precisely since no one could imaging that the latter represent the actual route taken by the letter.

Both of the cases are misleding for some reason, but it is good to experiment in order to understand what you gain and what you loose in every different attempt.
