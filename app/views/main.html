Objectives

A digital framework for multi-lateral collaboration

Between 1500 and 1800, a revolution in postal communication allowed ordinary men and women to scatter letters across and beyond Europe. This exchange helped knit together what contemporaries called the respublica litteraria, a knowledge-based civil society, crucial to that era’s intellectual breakthroughs, and formative of many modern European values and institutions.

Ironically, the exchange of letters which created this community also dispersed the documentation required to study it, posing enormous difficulties for historians of the subject ever since.

To reassemble that scattered material and chart the history of that imagined community we need a revolution in digital communications.

This COST Action is dedicated to envisaging the open-access, open-source, transnational digital infrastructure capable of facilitating the radically multilateral collaboration needed to reassemble this scattered documentation and to support a new generation of scholarly methods and research questions.

Geographical Extent

Researchers from 33 countries

COST Action IS1310 Reassembling the Republic of Letters, 1500–1800 is essentially a networking programme. A new scholarly network is needed in order to study the older scholarly network of the early modern period. To sustain this new network, we need a new breed of digital networking tools. The purpose of this Action is to assemble the network needed to design these tools: a network to devise a networking platform to support a network studying past networks.

At the heart of this Action as nearly 100 Management Committee Members and Substitute Members drawn from 32 of the 35 countries which make up COST. A growing number of Affiliates from even further afield also participate informally in the network.

Disciplinary Breadth

FOUR COMMUNITIES

In order to fulfil its task, the Action must attract experts not only from all quarters of Europe but also from several different areas of expertise. Scholars from many different fields predominate, since they set most of the intellectual agenda. Librarians and archivists best understand the task of cataloguing and preserving letters in script and print. Experts in many branches of information technology are vital to designing the infrastructure which these scholarly communities need. Visualisation and communication expertise is needed to reveal meaningful patterns in large quantities of data.

Structure

Six Working Groups

At the centre of this Action are six Working Groups, the scope of which is determined by the basic features of letters themselves. Letters are written documents sent by a one or more person(s) at a specific time and place to one or more other person(s) in another place.

The first three working groups deal with the (1) temporal and spatial, (2) biographical and social, and (3) textual and topical dimensions of letters. The others working groups handle (4) the material properties of letters and their archival histories, (5) the technical challenge of exchanging data within participating projects and institutions, and (6) the challenges of analysing and exploring complex data visually.
